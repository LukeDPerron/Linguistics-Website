Compounding

Compounding

Definition

A method of word formation in which two bases combine to form a new lexical item. The two bases may exist as independent words.
In other words, compounding creates a new morpheme from two otherwise free morphemes.

Characterization

Compounding and affixation can work together in word formation. A base in a compound may be affixed to the new lexical item to give it verb-hood, noun-hood, or other grammatical functions (Katamba 67). Most compounds are compound nouns, though compound verbs, adjectives, and adverbs also exist. One notable phonological feature of compounds is stress placement: stress typically falls on the first element of the compound rather than the second.

For example:
the `White 'house vs. a 'white `house

In the compound White House, stress falls on the first element, whereas in the phrase white house, stress falls on the second element (Katamba 68). Similar contrasts can be seen in pairs such as wet suit, hot dog, and black bird (Katamba 68).

In English, most compounds are headed, meaning that the syntactic category of the compound is determined by one of its bases—typically the second base (Katamba 67).

For example, blackberry is composed of the adjective black and the noun berry. Since berry heads the compound, blackberry is a noun (Katamba 67).

The second base also functions as the semantic head. The first base serves as a descriptor or specifier. In shoestring, both bases are nouns, but shoe describes the type of string rather than the compound referring to a shoe. Compounds with semantic heads are known as endocentric compounds. Compounds without semantic heads are called exocentric compounds. For instance, egghead does not refer to a type of head, but to an intellectually inclined person—there is no semantic head in this compound.

Another classification distinguishes root compounds from synthetic compounds. In root compounds (also called primary compounds), the head is not derived from a verb. The most common and productive type is the noun + noun construction, such as doghouse, strawberry, and airplane. In synthetic compounds, the head is deverbal and functions as a verb, while the first element acts as its object (Nordquist). Synthetic compounding combines both derivation and compounding.

Examples include:
noun + verb-er: dishwasher, watchmaker
noun + verb-ing: housekeeping, letter-writing
