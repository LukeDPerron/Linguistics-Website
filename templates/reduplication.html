Reduplication

Definition

A word formation process in which the base word is repeated either partially or entirely.

Characterization

The most significant property of reduplication are phonological factors (Katamba 73). How the base sounds in repetition, either partial or complete, is key in its productivity.

There are two different types of reduplication: partial and complete. In the complete duplication formation, the entire base is duplicated, such as pooh-pooh (Mattews 135). Partial reduplication occurs in a variety of forms. The vowel of the base can be lengthened, the consonant doubled, or the entire base nearly copied (Rubino). Many complete reduplications come from "baby-talk." Parents tend to introduce certain words to their infants via complete reduplication. Everything from ma-ma-, da-da, bye-bye-, ba-ba, and night-night are terms often said to babies to simplify the language and prompt understanding.

There are also rhyme motivated compounds and ablaut motivated compounds. In rhyme motivated compounds, both words making up the compound rhyme (Katamba 73). Most rhyming reduplications are formed by both bases not qualifying as independent words (Katamba 73). There are rhyme motivated compounds that are formed by both bases being pre-existing words, such as black-jack and brain-drain (Katamba 73). Ablaut motivated compounds signal a change in the root vowel (Katamba 73). Katamba notes that usually ablaut reduplications indicate a change in grammatical function (Katamba 73). People also use reduplication to emphasize contrast in what is called contrastive focus reduplication (Ghomeshi 307). In this process, the reduplication "is used as one way to clarify such situations, by specifying a prototypical denotation of the lexical item in contrast to a potentially looser or more specialized reading" (Ghomeshi 311). Take the following examples:

It's tuna salad, not salad-salad (Ghomeshi 311)

You're not sick-sick, are you?

He's not a dog-dog, he's a Pomeranian.

Notice in the above examples the reduplications are clarifying the category of the object of the sentence. A typical salad is not a tuna salad and the model dog is not a small, cute Pomeranian. Thus, the reduplication is simplifying their intended use of the object's category.

Lastly, reduplication can be used to get a point across in what's called intensive reduplication (Ghomeshi 309). In this type of reduplication the same base is repeated, usually three times, to convey a strong message. The following examples illustrate this point.

All you think about is you, you, you.

He kept telling himself, win, win, win.

Rain, rain, rain- all week!

The repetition of the base fixates and intensifies the message.
