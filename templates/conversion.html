Conversion

Conversion

Definition

The transition of a word from one part of speech to another part of speech.

Characterization

Conversion is also known as zero derivation. In this type of word formation, there is no change in the shape of the input base (Katamba 64). The only alteration the word undergoes is a semantic shift in its lexical categorization.

Conversion of nouns into verbs and verbs into nouns is highly productive in English (Katamba 64). Because the word form remains unchanged, grammatical context is often the only way to determine the word’s part of speech (Katamba 64). In the example below, like functions as both a noun and a verb. In its noun usage, the word undergoes conversion through zero derivation—no affix is added—demonstrating a key trait of conversion (Katamba 65).

Although verb-to-noun and noun-to-verb conversions are the most common, adjectives and adverbs can also undergo conversion. For instance, the word stupid can function as both an adjective and a noun without any change in form.

Examples

• backdoor (N → V): Doing something in a sneaky, secret, or unethical way to sidestep rules or authority to achieve a goal. Less than a week later, the Buckeyes are in great position to become the second team in program history to backdoor into the playoff without winning a conference championship.

• big timed (Adj + N → V): Meaning someone achieved fame and then ignored former friendships due to newfound success. He big timed us.

• private message (N → V): The act of sending someone a private message. You can private message me.

• no-show (N → V): The act of not appearing where one is expected. Michigan DT Mazi Smith no-shows media session at NFL Combine.

• maple sugaring (N → Adj): An event involving the production and celebration of maple sugar. March is packed with maple sugaring events at southeastern Michigan Metroparks.

• stretchered (N → V): The act of being carried off on a stretcher. Spanish hurdler Enrique Llopis stretchered off after horror fall at European Indoor Championships.

• bagel (N → V): The act of making bagels. In an episode of Seinfeld, Kramer says, “Toss me an apron, let’s bagel.”

• achoo’ed (Interjection → V): The act of sneezing while saying “achoo!” He achoo’ed right into the pan.

• too smalled (Adj → V): Used in basketball when one player is physically overpowered by another. LeBron James gets “too smalled” by Patrick Beverley.

• fundraise (N → V): The act of raising money. “Trump and Republicans fundraise off of indictment news.”
