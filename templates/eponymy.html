Eponymy

Definition

An eponymy is a type of word formation in which words are formed from proper nouns. These proper nouns are usually formed from people or places, fictitious or actual.

Characterization

Eponymy works together with other word formation processes to achieve new word formations. The most common of these processes conjoined with eponymy is conversion. Proper nouns are converted into a common nouns. Take the following three examples:

Leotard- named after French acrobat Jules Leotard

Jules Leotard(Proper N)>>>leotard(common N)

(Lalić 64)

Pavlova- named after Russian ballerina Anna Pavlova

Anna Pavlova(Proper N)>>>pavlova(common N)

(Lalić 64)

Silhouette- named after French finance minister Étienne de Silhouette

Étienne de Silhouette(Proper N)>>>silhouette(common N)

(Lalić 64)

Many eponyms are further lexically transformed into verbs. Take the noun and verb boycott, meaning to actively ostracize an opposing viewpoint or polarizing figure(s) one regards as unfair. The etymology of boycott originated in 1880 Ireland (Britannica). During the first land agitation, rent was increasing and land evictions were causing turmoil throughout the country (Britannica). Charles Stewart Parnell began a public shunning of British estate manager, Charles Cunningham Boycott (Britannica). Irish tenants followed Parnell in an active boycott (Britannica). We see the transition of boycott from Boycott(proper N), boycott(N), to boycott(v).

Eponyms in the science and medical fields are highly effective. This is because doctors and scientists who discover a disease, treatment, procedure, etc., tend to name their discoveries after themselves (Anderson 174). Examples of this are Bell's Palsy, named after Scottish surgeon and neuroanatomist Sir Charles Bell, fallopian tube named after anatomist Gabriel Fallopius, and Petri dish invented by the German bacteriologist Julius R. Petri (Anderson 174).
