Affixation

Definition

Affixation is the morphological process by which an affix (a bound morpheme) attaches to a morphological root (free morpheme).

Characterization

There are two types of affixes that are used to create new words: prefixes and suffixes. Prefixes are, usually, bound morphemes that occur before the root and suffixes are bound morphemes that occur after.

New word formations can occur in both prefixes and suffixes. However, we see suffixes used for new word formations more frequently than prefixes. Suffixes can alter a word's lexical category (Katamba 59). For example, inhabit is a verb meaning to occupy a space. If we add the suffix -ant, we get the noun inhabitant meaning a person or thing that occupies a space. Likewise if we have the noun doubt meaning to not fully believe in something and add the suffix -ful, we get the adjective doubtful. Suffixes can also modify a V to ADJ, N to V, ADJ to V, ADJ to ADV, and even a N to N (million to millionaire), V to V (chat to chattering) and ADJ to ADJ (blue to bluish) (Katamba 59-62).

An example of a new word formed by a prefix is "re-re-remake," whereas the bound morpheme -re is attached to the beginning of "make," several times, to illustrate something being remade three times. As in, DoorDash had to re-re-remake my order, which was irritating. An example of a new word forming by suffixation is "annoyedly." The bound morpheme is attached to the end of the word "annoyed," shifting the word into an adverb. He annoyedly paid for the $10 carton of eggs.
