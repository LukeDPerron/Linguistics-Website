Compounding

Definition

A method of word formation where two bases combine to form a new lexical item. The two bases may be words independently.
In other words, compounding creates a new morpheme from two otherwise free morphemes.

Characterization

Most commonly, source words belong to the same syntactic category (e.g., noun + noun), although blends from different syntactic categories do exist (Beliaeva 5). Source words—the independent words that merge to form the blend—often share phonological or graphical similarities (Beliaeva 3). The most common pattern in English blending involves taking the first part of the first source word and combining it with the second part of the second source word (Beliaeva 13). An example of this is frenemy, formed from friend and enemy.

Although there is no strict rule governing blend formation, several common factors influence the ordering of source words:

• Length of source words (shorter word comes first):
stay + vacation → staycation

• Frequency (more frequently used word comes first):
emotion + icon → emoticon

• Prototypicality (more prototypical word comes first):
spoon + fork → spork

• Pragmatics (order of real-world events):
breakfast + lunch → brunch (Beliaeva 8)

Phonological convenience and spelling also influence blend formation, though these factors are difficult to define precisely. However, blend types themselves are easier to categorize. The three most widely accepted types of blending are phonemic overlap, clipping, and a combination of overlap and clipping (Hosseinzadeh 19). Overlap occurs when a syllable or part of a syllable is shared between both source words, allowing the shared element to function in the new blend (Hosseinzadeh 19). For example, slanguage combines slang and language, which share the syllable lang (Hosseinzadeh 20).

Other overlap examples include:
sex + expert → sexpert
alcohol + holiday → alcoholiday

Clipping, the most common blending technique, removes part of one or more source words. The most frequent form is back clipping, where the front of the word is retained (Hosseinzadeh 19).

Other forms include:
• Fore clipping (retaining the back of the word), as in chute from parachute
• Middle clipping, as in fridge from refrigerator (Hosseinzadeh 20)

Some blends use both overlap and clipping simultaneously. An example is pomato, formed from potato and tomato. The shared syllable ato overlaps both words, while potato is back clipped to po and tomato is middle clipped to om (Hosseinzadeh 23).

Examples

• brunch (breakfast + lunch)

• smog (smoke + fog)

• motel (motor + hotel)

• infotainment (information + entertainment)

• spork (spoon + fork)

• frenemy (friend + enemy)

• glamping (glamorous + camping)

• hangry (hungry + angry)

• jeggings (jeans + leggings)

• Bollywood (Bombay + Hollywood)
